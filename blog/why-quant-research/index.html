Why I Chose Quantitative Research Over Software Engineering

Published: June 15, 2026 Updated: Jun 22, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read

The Mathematics Question

I grew up doing math olympiads. I qualified for INMO through RMO 2019, cleared INAO, and placed in the national top 1% in chemistry and physics olympiads. KVPY SA at AIR 148 was another confirmation that mathematics was not just a subject I studied — it was how I thought about problems.

Four years at IIT Kanpur reinforced this. Topology, discrete mathematics, probability theory, computational complexity — every A* on my transcript came from courses that demanded mathematical reasoning, not just engineering execution. When I sat in the machine learning course (CS771), I found myself more interested in the optimization theory behind gradient descent than in the TensorFlow API.

Software engineering, at most companies, asks you to build reliable systems. That is important work. But it rarely asks you to derive the solution from first principles. Quantitative research does.

The Right Kind of Hard

The interview process for quant firms is telling. At Barclays, they asked me to derive PCA from eigenvalue decomposition. At Morgan Stanley, they wanted expected payoff calculations for sequential dice rolls. At Trexquant, the conversation was about logistic regression loss functions, covariance matrices, and the precise meaning of multiclass classification from a statistical perspective.

These were not coding puzzles. They were mathematical reasoning problems with computational components — exactly the kind of thinking I had trained for since I was solving geometry problems at AIMED and AMTI competitions in middle school.

Software engineering interviews test data structures and system design. Quant interviews test whether you can think. For someone with my background, the choice was clear.

What Research at Adobe Taught Me

My internship at Adobe Research was formative. Working on multi-agent LLM optimization, I saw firsthand what research engineering looks like: systematic experiments, literature reviews, hypothesis testing, and measurable results (35% token reduction, 1.5× latency improvement). This was not about shipping features — it was about understanding a system deeply enough to improve it fundamentally.

Quantitative research at a firm like Squarepoint is similar in spirit: understand market microstructure, model it rigorously, and build systems that execute on those models. The difference is that the feedback loop is measured in P&L rather than benchmarks.

The Honest Tradeoffs

Quant research is not for everyone. The hours can be intense. The signal-to-noise ratio in financial data is brutally low. And unlike software engineering, you cannot always Google your way out of a problem — the problems are often novel, and the solutions require genuine mathematical creativity.

But for someone who spent their teenage years grinding through RMO problem sets and KVPY papers, who ranked first in Advent of Code at IIT Kanpur by solving problems in C++ for fun, and who gravitates toward mathematical rigor over API familiarity — this is the right kind of hard.

I am joining Squarepoint Capital after graduating from IIT Kanpur in 2026. If you want to discuss quantitative research, competitive programming, or anything at the intersection of math and engineering — reach out.
