Advent of Code 2024: 50 Stars and a Lot of Coffee

Published: Jan 2025 ⏱ 8 min read

If you're not familiar with it, Advent of Code is a yearly programming puzzle calendar. From December 1st to 25th, Eric Wastl drops a two-part algorithmic puzzle every day at exactly midnight EST (which translates to roughly 10:30 AM IST). The goal? Solve it as fast as possible.

The Setup

By December 2024, I was in my pre-final year vacations at IIT Kanpur. I decided it was a great time to compete against 200+ other folks at IITK on our private leaderboard.

I had my C++ template ready, my Python environment configured for when string parsing got too annoying in C++, and a concerning amount of coffee.

The First Week: Speed is Everything

The first 7 days are always a test of reading comprehension and typing speed. The algorithmic complexity is low — usually basic string manipulation, hash maps, or simple simulation. The real challenge is not making stupid off-by-one errors.

During these days, my Codeforces background (hovering around 1729 rating) carried me. When you're used to competitive programming, writing a DFS or a BFS takes less time than reading the problem description. I managed to secure top 3 on the IITK leaderboard almost every day in the first week.

The Middle: Where Graphs Reign Supreme

Around Day 10, things start getting spicy. We saw 3D geometry, cellular automata, and the inevitable shortest-path problems. Day 16 was particularly brutal — a dynamic programming problem disguised as a graph traversal. I spent a good hour debugging a memoization state that was slightly too large for my cache.

This is where Python became a liability. While it's great for parsing the weird inputs Eric gives us, running a BFS on a 1000x1000 grid in Python is just asking for a Time Limit Exceeded. I fully committed to C++ from this point on.

The Endgame: Pure Math and Optimization

Days 20 to 25 are the real separator. They almost always require recognizing a mathematical property of the input. In 2024, there was a problem involving modular arithmetic and the Chinese Remainder Theorem, which was a beautiful throwback to my Olympiad days.

The final push was Day 24, a massive system of equations that needed to be solved using Z3 (a theorem prover). Some people wrote manual solvers, but realizing that Z3 could handle it in 5 lines of Python was a game-changer. That insight alone pushed me to the #1 spot on the leaderboard.

Takeaways

Finishing 1st at IIT Kanpur was incredibly satisfying, but the real reward was the sheer fun of it. Advent of Code doesn't just test your algorithms; it tests your ability to think clearly under time pressure, use the right tool for the job, and appreciate the elegance of a well-crafted puzzle.

Will I do it again? Probably. Will I regret losing sleep over it? Absolutely not.
